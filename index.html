Green's Theorem

Statement (circulation form)

∮C P dx + Q dy = ∬D (∂Q/∂x − ∂P/∂y) dA

Let C be a positively oriented (counter-clockwise), piecewise-smooth, simple closed curve in the plane and D the region it encloses. If P and Q have continuous first partial derivatives on an open region containing D, the circulation of F = (P, Q) around C equals the double integral of the scalar curl over D.

Flux (divergence) form

∮C F · n ds = ∬D (∂P/∂x + ∂Q/∂y) dA

Replacing the tangential component by the outward normal component turns Green's theorem into a 2-D divergence theorem: the outward flux across the boundary equals the total divergence inside.

Area by line integral

A = ½ ∮C (x dy − y dx) = ∮C x dy = −∮C y dx

Choosing fields whose curl is 1 lets you compute the area of a region purely from its boundary — the principle behind the mechanical planimeter.

Hypotheses & pitfalls

The curve must be simple (no self-intersections), closed, and traversed counter-clockwise; clockwise orientation flips the sign. P and Q must be C¹ on all of D: the field F = (−y, x)/(x² + y²) has zero curl away from the origin, yet circulation 2π around it — the singularity at the origin breaks the hypotheses.

Stokes' Theorem

Statement

∮C F · dr = ∬S (∇×F) · dS

Let S be an oriented, piecewise-smooth surface bounded by a simple, closed, piecewise-smooth curve C with positive orientation (right-hand rule: fingers along C, thumb along the normal n). For a C¹ vector field F, the circulation around the boundary equals the flux of the curl through the surface.

Curl — meaning

∇×F = (∂Fz/∂y − ∂Fy/∂z, ∂Fx/∂z − ∂Fz/∂x, ∂Fy/∂x − ∂Fx/∂y)

The curl measures micro-rotation: a tiny paddle wheel placed in the flow spins fastest about the axis of ∇×F, at angular speed ½|∇×F|. A field with ∇×F = 0 everywhere is called irrotational.

Surface independence

Any two oriented surfaces sharing the same boundary curve give the same flux of curl — hemisphere, paraboloid or flat disk. Consequence: the flux of a curl through any closed surface is zero (a closed surface has empty boundary).

Applications & special cases

Faraday's law: ∮ E · dr = −dΦB/dt — a changing magnetic flux induces a circulating electric field. Ampère's law: ∮ B · dr = μ₀ Ienc. When S lies flat in the xy-plane, Stokes' theorem reduces exactly to Green's theorem.

Divergence Theorem

Statement (Gauss–Ostrogradsky)

∬S F · dS = ∭V (∇·F) dV

Let V be a solid region bounded by a closed, piecewise-smooth surface S oriented by the outward normal. For a C¹ field F, the net outward flux through S equals the total divergence inside V.

Divergence — meaning

∇·F = ∂Fx/∂x + ∂Fy/∂y + ∂Fz/∂z

Divergence is the source density: positive where fluid is created (a source), negative where it disappears (a sink). A field with ∇·F = 0 is incompressible (solenoidal) — whatever flows into any region flows back out.

Applications

Gauss's law: ∬ E · dS = Qenc/ε₀. Continuity equation: ∂ρ/∂t + ∇·(ρv) = 0 — conservation of mass in fluid flow. Also the divergence form of the heat equation and the derivation of Archimedes' buoyancy force.

Hypotheses & pitfalls

S must be closed and oriented outward; F must be C¹ throughout V. The inverse square field F = r̂/r² has ∇·F = 0 away from the origin, yet flux 4π through any surface enclosing it — the origin singularity must be excluded (this is exactly how Gauss's law encodes a point charge).

Gradient Theorem

Statement (FT of line integrals)

∫C ∇f · dr = f(B) − f(A)

If C is any piecewise-smooth curve from A to B and f is a C¹ scalar field, the line integral of its gradient depends only on the endpoints — never on the route taken. It is the direct generalization of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus to curves.

Conservative fields — equivalences

For a C¹ field F on an open, simply connected region, these are equivalent:

  • F = ∇f for some potential f (F is conservative)
  • ∫ F · dr is path-independent
  • ∮ F · dr = 0 around every closed loop
  • ∇×F = 0 (in 2-D: ∂Q/∂x = ∂P/∂y)

Applications

Work–energy: for a conservative force, W = −ΔU — define potential energy U = −f and work done is independent of the path. Gravity and the electrostatic field are conservative; friction and magnetic forces on moving charges are not.

Pitfall — holes matter

On a region with a hole (not simply connected), ∇×F = 0 does not imply conservative: the swirl F = (−y, x)/(x² + y²) is curl-free on the punctured plane but has circulation 2π around the origin. Topology decides whether a potential exists.

The Big Picture

Generalized Stokes theorem

∫∂Ω ω = ∫Ω dω

All four theorems (and the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus) are one statement in the language of differential forms: the integral of a form ω over the boundary ∂Ω equals the integral of its exterior derivative dω over the region Ω. Interior contributions telescope; only the boundary survives.

The dictionary

FTC — interval [a,b], boundary {a,b}: ∫f′dx = f(b) − f(a).
Gradient — curve, boundary = endpoints: ∫∇f·dr = f(B) − f(A).
Green — plane region, boundary = closed curve.
Stokes — surface in 3-D, boundary = closed curve.
Divergence — solid volume, boundary = closed surface.

Operator identities

∇×(∇f) = 0  ·  ∇·(∇×F) = 0  ·  ∇·∇f = ∇²f

Curl of a gradient vanishes (gradients don't swirl); divergence of a curl vanishes (curls have no sources). These are the reason d(dω) = 0 in the language of forms, and they generate the sequences behind potential theory and the Helmholtz decomposition F = −∇φ + ∇×A.

A little history

George Green (1828) published his theorem in a self-financed essay on electricity and magnetism. Lord Kelvin communicated the surface theorem to George Stokes, who set it as a Cambridge exam problem (1854). Gauss and Ostrogradsky proved the divergence theorem for flux of fluids and gravitation. Élie Cartan (1945) unified them all with differential forms.

One theorem to rule them all — the Generalized Stokes Theorem
∫∂Ω ω  =  ∫Ω dω

Every theorem in this explorer is the same statement: the total of a derivative over a region equals the total of the original quantity over the region's boundary. The interior "micro-changes" (dω) telescope — everything cancels except what happens at the edge (∂Ω).

TheoremRegion ΩBoundary ∂ΩStatement
Fundamental Thm. of Calculus interval [a, b]two points {a, b} ∫ab f′(x) dx = f(b) − f(a)
Gradient Theorem curve C in spaceendpoints A, B ∫C ∇f · dr = f(B) − f(A)
Green's Theorem plane region Dclosed curve C ∮C P dx + Q dy = ∬D (Qx − Py) dA
Stokes' Theorem surface S in 3Dclosed curve C ∮C F · dr = ∬S (∇×F) · dS
Divergence Theorem solid volume Vclosed surface S ∬S F · dS = ∭V (∇·F) dV
Why does this pattern exist? Chop the region Ω into tiny pieces. On each piece, the derivative (curl, divergence, gradient…) measures the field's imbalance across that piece's own tiny boundary. When you add the pieces back together, every interior wall is shared by two neighbors with opposite orientations — their contributions cancel perfectly. The only walls with no partner are on the outer boundary ∂Ω. So the sum of all microscopic boundary effects telescopes into one macroscopic boundary integral. In dimension 1 that's the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus; in dimensions 2 and 3 it becomes the four theorems you just explored; in the language of differential forms it is a single equation, ∫∂Ω ω = ∫Ω dω.
